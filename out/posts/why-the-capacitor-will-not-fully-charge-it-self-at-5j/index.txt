<p>we learnt that when the time is equal to 5J the capacitor must be fully charged.</p>
<p>in the calculation it gave us the capacitor will be fully charged in 0.25s but is wasn&#39;t (100%) fully charged it was 11.923v and was supposed to be 12v the error is (11.923-12/12)*100 = 0.6%.<br>the error witch is 0.6% wasn&#39;t supposed to be why is it there?</p>
<p>in the first place the 5J doesn&#39;t mean the capacitor fully charged it meant the capacitor reached 99.3% and we can prove that with the number we have from the simulation witch (11.923/12)*100% = 99.33% </p>
<p>in theory we are fine with this approximation because in the real world the voltage in the capacitor can&#39;t be 100% fully charged </p>
<p>the function of the capacitor $V(t) = V_{max}(1-e^{-t/RC})$ </p>
<ul>
<li>$V_{max}$ is the maximum voltage the capacitor can charge to (12 V in your case) </li>
<li>$t$ is the time elapsed</li>
<li>$RC$ is the time constant of the circuit<br><img src="/my-next-blog/images/Pasted%20image%2020250121015614.png" alt=""></li>
</ul>
<p>It would take an infinite amount of time for the capacitor to reach exactly 12V because $e^{-t/RC}$ approaches zero asymptotically<br><img src="/my-next-blog/images/Pasted%20image%2020250121015737.png" alt=""></p>
<p>but also in the simulation it showed that the voltage across the capacitor reach the maximum witch is 12v witch we already said it should not reach it<br><img src="/my-next-blog/images/Pasted%20image%2020250121020039.png" alt=""></p>
<p>ok why it reached the max?</p>
<ul>
<li>Oscilloscopes typically display voltages with a limited precision, and when the voltage is close enough (e.g., within microvolts of 12 V), the device rounds the value to 12 V.</li>
<li>In reality, the voltage might still be a tiny fraction below 12 V (e.g., 11.999999 V), but the oscilloscope doesn&#39;t display that level of granularity.<br>The higher the resolution, the smaller the voltage differences the oscilloscope can detect and display.</li>
</ul>
